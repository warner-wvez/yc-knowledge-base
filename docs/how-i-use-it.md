# How I use it

A knowledge base is only worth building if it actually changes what you do. This one does. So here is the real use, without the jargon.

---

## The problem it solves

The YC Startup Library is excellent, and it is free. It is also hundreds of hours of video and dozens of long essays, with no connection between any of it. And if you watch enough of it, you end up in a very familiar spot. You know that someone said something sharp about when to raise money, or how to price a first product, or how to talk to your early users, but you simply cannot find it again. It is buried somewhere around minute 38 of a podcast you watched two months ago.

Advice you cannot re-find is not really advice you have.

---

## What I do instead

When I am building and I hit a real decision, I go to the brain and I ask it a question. Not a vague one, a specific one tied to the exact choice in front of me. From there, the structure does its job:

1. **Search the graph** for the question. Full-text search runs across every note at once, so the talks and essays that actually matter surface together instead of one at a time.
2. **Read the summaries** of the handful that genuinely speak to it. Every note carries a short summary up top, so I can tell in seconds whether a source is on point before I commit to reading it.
3. **Follow the links.** From any note, one click takes me to the author's page, which lists everything else they have put out, and one click takes me to the series, which holds the related entries. A good source leads straight to its neighbors.
4. **Form a view I can actually defend.** By the end I am holding a position backed by named people and real sources, with a link back to each original, instead of a gut feel or a half-memory.

The point is not that the brain makes the decision for me. It does not. The point is that I am deciding against the best available evidence in minutes, and I can always trace any claim back to who said it and exactly where.

---

## The kinds of questions it answers

The library covers a wide span of company building, so the brain gets used across most of it. Here is a sample of the kinds of questions, kept general on purpose:

- Fundraising: why, when, and how much to raise, and what investors are actually grading.
- Early product: what to build first, what to cut, and how to tell if it is working.
- Pricing and business model: how founders have thought about charging, and the common traps.
- Growth and conversion: real benchmarks, and what actually moves them.
- Hiring and team: when to hire, who to hire first, and how lean to stay.
- Talking to users: how to learn from customers without fooling yourself.
- Knowing when to change course versus when to push harder.

For any one of these, the brain turns "what has actually been said about this" into a ten second lookup with named sources, instead of a vague recollection or a fresh internet search that starts from zero.

---

## Why the structure matters here

None of this works without the structure laid out in [How it was built](how-it-was-built.md). The reason a single question returns a clean, connected answer comes down to three things:

- Every note has a **summary**, so I can triage sources fast.
- Every note links to its **author and series**, so one good hit opens the door to its neighbors.
- Every note carries the same **metadata**, so I can slice the library by person, by series, or by type without writing any code.

A folder of 466 unconnected transcripts would do none of this. The same content, once it is structured, becomes something you can actually think with.

---

## The honest version

I am a solo founder and business owner. I do not have a team to bounce decisions off of, and I cannot afford to relearn the same lessons the expensive way. So this is how I borrow the experience of people who have already done it. I made their advice findable, connected, and fast, so that it is sitting right there at the exact moment I need it. And that, all in all, is the whole use case.
