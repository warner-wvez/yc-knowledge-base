# How I use it

A knowledge base is only worth building if it changes what you do. This one does. Here is the actual use, without the jargon.

---

## The problem it solves

The YC Startup Library is excellent and free. It is also hundreds of hours of video and dozens of long essays, with no connection between them. If you watch a lot of it, you end up in a familiar spot: you know someone said something sharp about, say, when to raise money, or how to price a first product, or how to talk to early users, but you cannot find it again. It is buried at minute 38 of a podcast you watched two months ago.

Advice you cannot re-find is advice you do not really have.

---

## What I do instead

When I am building and I hit a real decision, I go to the brain and ask it a question. Not a vague one, a specific one tied to the choice in front of me. Then the structure does its job:

1. **Search the graph** for the question. Full-text search runs across every note at once, so the relevant talks and essays surface together instead of one at a time.
2. **Read the summaries** of the handful that actually speak to it. Each note has a short summary up top, so I can tell in seconds whether a source is on point before committing to it.
3. **Follow the links.** From any note, one click goes to the author's page, which lists everything else they have said, and one click goes to the series, which holds related entries. A good source leads to its neighbors.
4. **Form a view I can defend.** At the end I have a position backed by named people and real sources, with a link back to each original, instead of a gut feel or a half-memory.

The point is not that the brain makes the decision. It does not. The point is that I am deciding against the best available evidence in minutes, and I can always trace any claim back to who said it and where.

---

## The kinds of questions it answers

The library covers a wide span of company building, so the brain gets used across all of it. A sample of the question types, kept general on purpose:

- Fundraising: why, when, and how much to raise, and what investors are actually grading.
- Early product: what to build first, what to cut, and how to tell if it is working.
- Pricing and business model: how founders have thought about charging, and common traps.
- Growth and conversion: real benchmarks and what moves them.
- Hiring and team: when to hire, who to hire first, and how lean to stay.
- Talking to users: how to learn from customers without fooling yourself.
- Knowing when to change course versus push harder.

For any of these, the brain turns "what has actually been said about this" into a ten second lookup with named sources, instead of a vague recollection or a fresh internet search that starts from zero.

---

## Why the structure matters here

None of this works without the structure described in [How it was built](how-it-was-built.md). The reason a question returns a clean, connected answer is that:

- Every note has a **summary**, so I can triage sources fast.
- Every note links to its **author and series**, so one good hit opens the door to its neighbors.
- Every note carries the same **metadata**, so I can slice the library by person, by series, or by type without any code.

A folder of 466 unconnected transcripts would not do any of this. The same content, structured, becomes something you can actually think with.

---

## The honest version

I am a solo founder. I do not have a team to bounce decisions off, and I cannot afford to relearn the same lessons the expensive way. This is how I borrow the experience of people who have already done it: I made their advice findable, connected, and fast, so it is there at the exact moment I need it. That is the whole use case.
